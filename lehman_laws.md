# Lehman's Laws - Laws of software evolution

1. Continuing changes: a system must be continually adapted or it becomes progressively less satisfactory in use
   - Software that doesn't change dies

2. Increasing complexity: as a system evolves, its complexity increases unless work is done to maintain or reduce it
   - Software that changes without discipline rots

3. Self-regulation: the evolution process is self-regulating, with close to normal distributions of measures of product and process
   - Software projects tend to regulate themselves toward an average pace of change over time
     - Teams rarely release 10x more features one month and nothing on the next
     - Bug rates, change rates, and complexity growth all tend to stabilize around a "normal" for that system and organization

Meaning, you can't sustainably force a system to evolve much faster or slower than its natural rhythm without consequences:

- burnout
- quality collapse
- stagnation

4. Conservation of organizational stability: the average effective global activity rate in an evolving system is invariant over the product's lifetime

Meaning, the overall pace of development in a project tends to stay roughly constant over time, regardless of how many people you add or how much pressure you apply

- Brook's law: adding people to a late project makes it later

5. Conservation of familiarity: familiarity with the system content must be maintained over its lifetime to support satisfactory evolution

> Lehman's laws describe what happens when you don't fight entropy. AI doesn't remove entropy, it accelerates it. The laws don't break, they happen faster. So the teams that will benefit the most from its use, are teams that use AI to reduce complexity rather than just shipping faster. So, better refactoring, faster test coverage, clearer documentation will be more and more important in the AI age.
